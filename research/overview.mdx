## Motivation

Acoustic particle agglomeration is a key mechanism in industrial PM2.5 removal, aerosol manipulation, and microfluidic separation. Traditional simulation pipelines rely on:

- **FEM** (e.g. COMSOL) to resolve the acoustic field, and
- **DEM** to integrate per-particle dynamics.

Both are accurate but prohibitively slow for parameter sweeps or real-time control. My research focuses on building a physics-informed neural surrogate that retains FEM-level accuracy while running orders of magnitude faster.

## Key contributions

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Factorized PINN architecture" icon="sitemap">
    Decomposes acoustic radiation force and Stokes drag into independent spatial, temporal, and velocity sub-networks, drastically reducing training cost.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Random Fourier features" icon="wave-square">
    Eliminates spectral bias so the network can faithfully represent high-frequency standing-wave patterns.
  </Card>

  <Card title="27x speedup vs. FEM" icon="gauge-high">
    Achieves sub-0.01% error against COMSOL baselines while reducing wall-clock time by more than an order of magnitude.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Open-source release" icon="github">
    Full training and inference pipeline released as FF-PINN, with pre-trained weight packs for common frequencies.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Research questions

1. Can a factorized PINN surrogate match FEM accuracy across a wide range of acoustic frequencies and SPLs?
2. How does Fourier-feature encoding scale with the wavelength-to-domain ratio?
3. What is the accuracy/runtime Pareto frontier compared with DEM and FEM?
